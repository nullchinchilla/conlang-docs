# Phonology

This describes the phonology of Classical Taaleva, which is still taught prescriptively as the standard pronunciation of Taaleva and used in formal contexts.

## Vowels

Phonemically, these are the vowels, as well as the standard pronunciation:

```
<i> [i]                 <u> [u]

    <e> [e]             <o> [o]

               <a> [a]
```

&#x20;/e/ and /o/ are mid vowels, not high-mid.

### Vowel length

There is a vowel-length distinction for /a/ /i/ /u/. Long vowels are written doubled: **taaleva**.

As with Sanskrit, /e/ and /o/ are _phonotactically inherently long_, due to them originating from diphthongs. Phonologically, they're long in initial open syllables and short otherwise.

## Consonants

The consonant system is _relatively_ conservative vis-a-vis the Taalic languages' Mandarin ancestor. The most notable sound change, which differentiates Common Taalic from all other branches, are the following interconnected sound changes:

* In all positions:
  * /ʂ/ /ɕ/ /tʂ/ /tɕ/ > /ɬ/ /tɬ/ > /l/
  * /tʂʰ/ /tɕʰ/ > /tɬʰ/ > /tɬ/
* In initial position:
  * /l/ > /n/
  * /pʰ/ /tʰ/ /kʰ/ /tsʰ/ > /p/ /t/ /k/ /ts/
* In medial position:
  * /l/ > /r/
  * /p/ /t/ /k/ /ts/ > /v/ /d/ /h/ /s/
  * /pʰ/ /tʰ/ /kʰ/ /tsʰ/ > /p/ /t/ /k/ /ts/

Thus, a word like **大爆炸 dàbàozhà** comes **tavola** _beginning, genesis_.

The glottal stop [ʔ] can reflect an underlying morphophoneme /Q/, which surfaces as gemination when followed by a consonant. e.g. the prefix oQ- derives superlatives, but surfaces as **oxaatle** *very safe* **oppaada** *very fat*.

## Prosody

The accented syllable is always the first syllable. If the accented syllable in a multisyllabic word is long, it receives a _falling_ accent, and if it is short, it receives a _slightly rising_ accent. This sounds essentially like the equivalent in Serbo-Croatian.

For instance:

**Taaleve otto nos yon.**\
\*当寨-边 \*好多 \*老子 \*有呢\
_There's a lot of people on Taale._\
\[tâː.le̩.ve̩ ǒ̩t.to̩ no̩ːs jo̩n]

## Where did the tones and final nasals go?

All tones and final nasals are gone from Mandarin. But the tones and nasals _did_ condition sound changes that produced long vowels and syllable-final glottal stops:

|      | \*ā                                               | \*á                                         | \*ǎ                                         | \*à                                      |
| ---- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| \*-N | <p>saalu &#x3C; *三数<br><em>three</em></p>         | <p>miisu &#x3C; *名族<br><em>noble</em></p>   | <p>taxla &#x3C; *胆志<br><em>emotion</em></p> | <p>tuuva &#x3C; *炖吧<br><em>bowl</em></p> |
| \*-Ø | <p>paavaa &#x3C; *霸王<br><em>king, prince</em></p> | <p>kae &#x3C; *可爱<br><em>beautiful</em></p> | <p>tivu &#x3C; *底部<br><em>down</em></p>     | <p>kul &#x3C; *故事<br><em>story</em></p> |

Note that the inherently long /o/ and /e/ behave like the \*-N series, e.g. **otto** < \*好多.
